Input Data - Turkey

This section details the input data utilized in applying the LP modeling framework to the case study of Turkey. The primary objective is to provide data for modelling a prospective Turkish energy system for the year 2035. Additionally, we provide the necessary data to reproduce the historical Turkish energy system for the year 2019, serving as a validation of EnergyScope’s accuracy in modeling this intricate system.

Since many data are common between the Colombian and Turkish case studies, we will only present the data and assumptions that differ between the two. If nothing is indicated, the same data and assumptions as for Colombia can be used.

Resources

Local renewable resources

The energy transition heavily relies on renewable energies, which makes their deployment potential a critical parameter. Yet, this potential was still very little used in 2019. As highlighted by IEA132, in 2019, the share of fossil fuels in total primary energy supply [of Turkey] was 83%, which ranked the ninth-highest among IEA member countries.

Electricity

Final electricity consumption in 2019 is taken from PFU_DATABASE. The electricity used for heating and cooling in 2019 is subtracted from it, based on the values given in PFU_DATABASE. This aggregated electricity EUD is then divided between baseload and variable load according to the proportions retrieved from the EPIAS Transparency Portal for the year 2019 (https://seffaflik.epias.com.tr/transparency/). This gives a share of 55% baseload and 45% variable load. Finally, the values for baseload and variable load are divided between the different economic sectors by using the proportions given in IEA137 (and aggregating together industry, agriculture and fishing).

For \(\%_{elec}\), we normalize the real electricity demand from the year 2019, available on the EPIAS Transparency Portal (https://seffaflik.epias.com.tr/transparency/).

Heating and cooling

The aggregated EUDs for different heating and cooling types were retrieved from PFU_DATABASE. The time series \(\%_{sh}\) and \(\%_{sc}\) are based on our own computations, following the method described in 23.

(62)\[\begin{split}HDD = \sum_{t \in \text{T}}(T_{comf}(t) - T_{out}(t))\quad\text{if}\quad T_{out}(t) < 15°C\\ HDD = 0\quad\text{if}\quad T_{out}(t) \geq 15°C\end{split}\]
(63)\[\begin{split}CDD = \sum_{t \in \text{T}}(T_{out}(t) - T_{comf}(t))\quad\text{if}\quad T_{out}(t) > 24°C\\ CDD = 0\quad\text{if}\quad T_{out}(t) \leq 24°C\end{split}\]

Hourly outdoor temperature time series were retrieved from 55 for the cities of Istanbul, Izmir, Ankara, Ordu, Sanliurfa, Van and Antalya. The HDD and CDD time series for these individual cities were then computed following eqs. (62) and (63). The HDD and CDD time series for Turkey were computed by taking a weighted average of these 7 time series, with weights (0.32, 0.13, 0.15, 0.09, 0.10, 0.08, 0.13). These weights were computed as proportional to the populations of the areas surrounding those cities.

Mobility

Aggregated numbers are retrieved from 143 for the demand for passenger mobility and freight. These numbers are only for road transportation. The demand for passenger mobility is therefore divided by 98% to obtain the total EUD for passenger mobility, which includes rail transport but excludes domestic aviation transport. Similarly, the demand for freight is divided by 96% to obtain the total EUD for freight, which includes rail transport but excludes coastal shipping, aviation and pipeline transport.

For \(\%_{pass}\), we assume that the passenger mobility EUD has the same profile for every day of the year. This daily profile is taken from data for Switzerland (data from Figure 12 of 56). For \(\%_{fr}\), we take a uniform value over the 8760 hours of the year.

Non-energy

Non-energy EUD value in 2019 is taken from IEA137. We assume it to be uniformly spread over the 8760 hours of the year.

Table 47 includes the values of the yearly capacity factor (\(c_p\)) of technologies. As described in the Model Formulation Section, the values of \(c_p\) for intermittent renewables is in fact equal to one, while it is the value of their hourly load factor, \(c_{p,t}\), which is binding. The value of \(c_p\) given in Table 47 for intermittent renewables is in fact the mean value of \(c_{p,t}\) over the year. The yearly profile (which sums up to one) of \(c_{p,t}\) for intermittent renewables is computed as follows.

Power production profile of PV [10] were retrieved from 55 for the cities of Istanbul, Izmir, Ankara, Ordu, Sanliurfa, Van and Antalya. The yearly profile of \(c_{p,t}\) for PV in Turkey was then computed by taking a weighted average of these 7 time series, with weights (0.32, 0.13, 0.15, 0.09, 0.10, 0.08, 0.13). These weights were taken proportional to the populations of the geographical regions encompassing these cities, given in 145.

The yearly profile of \(c_{p,t}\) for onshore wind was computed in the same way, retrieving from 55 power production profiles of wind turbines [11] instead of PV.

The same 7 time series as for onshore wind were used for offshore wind, but the weighted average to obtain the yearly profile of \(c_{p,t}\) was computed by using the weights (0.2,0.2,0,0.3,0,0,0.3). These weights are proportional to the length of coastline near each city, according to numbers from 146.

Regarding CSP, the (non-normalized) time series for \(c_{p,t}\) is computed with the oemof thermal 66 and pvlib 67 packages (to extract pvgis data 68). These time series give the thermal GWh of energy produced by thermal GW of Collector installed. These time series are computed for the 6 locations of respective (lat,lon): (37.72, 27.9), (37.38, 30.75), (37.73, 33.69), (38.45, 36.95), (38.12, 39.62) and (38.25, 43.08). These locations were identified as the ones having the highest CSP potential, based on the open-source database from 48. The weighted mean of these six time series is then computed, using the weights (0.05, 0.11, 0.3 , 0.13, 0.33, 0.09) which reflect the respective potentials of these different locations, according to 48. The obtained mean time series is the yearly \(c_{p,t}\) time series for CSP in Turkey in 2035.

Finally, for hydro dam and hydro river, daily incoming water flow to hydro-electric facilities in Turkey was taken from the Turkish TSO website for the 365 days of the year. These data were normalized to give a yearly profile \(c_{p,t}\), taking the same value for each hour of a same day.

Non-renewable

Table 48 gives the data for the non-renewable electricity generation technologies modelled in EnergyScope Turkey, together with their sources. The minimum installed capacity (\(f_{min}\)) is zero, while the maximum installed capacity (\(f_{max}\)) is set to a value high enough for each technology to potentially cover the entire demand - except for nuclear energy. The maximum (\(f_{max,\%}\)) and minimum (\(f_{min,\%}\)) shares are imposed to 0 and 100% respectively, i.e. they are not constraining the model. The efficiencies of each technology in 2019 and 2035 are given as well.

The values of (\(f_{min}\)) and (\(f_{max}\)) of nuclear energy are set differently. Indeed, the choice to build new nuclear power plants is not simply based on a cost-benefit analysis. It results from political decisions, often closely linked to international relations. We set \(f_{min} = 4.8\) [GW] and \(f_{max} = 7.2\) [GW] for nuclear power in Turkey in 2035. Indeed, the Akuyu power plant of capacity 4.8 [GW] should be operational by then 144. The value of 7.2 [GW] corresponds to the capacity envisaged in Turkey’s National Energy Plan for 2035 142, although no plan to build a new nuclear central besides Akuyu has yet been announced. We leave it to the cost optimization of EnergyScope to decide the capacity to be installed between those two bounds.

The \(c_{p,t}\) time series for solar thermal in Turkey is obtained by dividing this weighted average time series by 8760 (number of hours in the year) and by 1000 (standard irradiation). The mean capacity factor of solar thermal (i.e. the mean of its \(c_{p,t}\) time series) is thus found to be 20.8%.

Electricity grid

Several data regarding cross-border interconnections are given in Section Electricity imports and exports. We take the distance from capital city to capital city, and not the distance from border to border, to grossly reflect the local grid reinforcement costs that such new interconnection projects entail.

GHG emissions

As already explicited in Table 43, two CO 2-eq emissions metrics are used: CO 2,direct and gwp op. The first one relates to the direct emissions associated with the fuels’ combustion, while the second one is the GWP100a-IPCC2013 metric: it covers emissions associated to extraction, transportation and combustion. The former is used to complete the calibration of EnergyScope to the 2019 Turkish energy system, whereas the second one determines the maximum GHG emissions allowed in 2035.
